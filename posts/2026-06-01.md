For two years, "AI strategy" meant picking a chatbot vendor. That era just ended.

The shift nobody announced: in the last six months AI stopped being a feature you bolt on and became infrastructure you build on.

Three things made it real — not hype:

→ Protocols standardized. The way agents plug into your tools (MCP) became a vendor-neutral standard, now governed under the Linux Foundation. Your CRM, your ads, your analytics, your CMS all speak the same language to any model. HubSpot, Webflow, Stripe, GitHub already ship official connectors.

→ Models specialized. There's no "best AI" anymore — there's the right model per job. That's an architecture decision, not a subscription.

→ Agents got durable. They run on schedules, survive crashes, and hold context across sessions. Not a chat window you open — a service that runs.

For a marketing or growth team, the question changed completely.

It stopped being "which AI tool do we buy."
It became "what does our system do while we sleep."

At the PropTech SaaS where I run growth, the marketing function already runs this way: a model orchestrating scheduled jobs, wired into our CRM, ads, analytics and CMS, with a memory that keeps context across every session.

Not because it's futuristic. Because it's cheaper, faster, and it doesn't forget.

A feature waits for you to open it.
Infrastructure runs whether you show up or not.

That's the whole shift. The teams who get it are building systems. The rest are still buying subscriptions.
